Hmong Tradition
Social and Religious


Summary

Hmong people have a traditional system of belief, called by a few names: "Kev Cai Qub", meaing "the old ways", or "Kev Cai Dab Qhuas", meaning "the way of spirit worship", are the most common. In diaspora, many hmong people have converted to other religions such as Christianity, but few in Asia participate in the dominant religions of those areas. Traditional hmong religion revolves around ancestral spirits; family members who have departed but continue to live on in spirit. Worship is similar to that of confucianism in some senses, namely that shrines are placed in specific parts of the home and are used to burn incense. However, Hmong shamanism involves many more rituals, and a dependence on a shaman in order to complete those rituals. A shaman interacts with those in the spirit world on behalf of the living to heal illness and provide protection, often giving animal sacrifices as payment for services from the spirits. Most traditions involve warding off evil spirits, such as placing shiny paper on a front door so that evil spirits attempting to look in will instead see their reflection, and become convinced that nobody is there. Traditions are similar for animal horns, dormant wasp nests, and lion statues.

Instruments play an important part in Hmong ceremonies. The Qeej, pictured above, is a wind instrument used to communicate with the spirits of the departed. The music played on this instrument by a shaman is intended to guide spirits home after they die and also to call spirits to assist the living. Drums are also a common instrument used in hmong rituals, for similar reasons.

Cultural and social traditions are also evident in Hmong life; gatherings of hmong people are very common, with relatives from all over joining in. Hmong funerals and weddings are multiple day events, with a set of complicated ceremonies involved in each. For many hmong families, just about every weekend is packed with some family event or another. A major yearly event is the harvest festival called Hmong New Year. It is a time to bring people together and celebrate, as well as give offerings to the ancestors. The largest hmong new year in the world is held every year in Fresno, California. People from all over the world come to celebrate hmong culture and spend time with family. Smaller new years festivals are held in just about every community at different times, so none of them overlap. One could travel from new year to new year consecutively from September to January! Pictured above is a game called "Pov Pob", or "ball throwing", that is played at Hmong New Year. The purpose is for young men and women to meet and basically speed date while throwing a ball back and forth. This also shows some of the traditional hmong clothing used for festivals, full of color and with intricate patterns.
