Hmong Diaspora
Living in a New World

There are now hmong communities in countries all across the world; those who fled Laos in the aftermath of the Vietnam confilct left Asia to find homes in many other places. Some of the most populated hmong diaspora are a few in the United States, and also one in France. Hmong people came to these countries starting in 1973 as refugees. California, Minnesota, and Alaska have significant Hmong populations, and the communities do their best to keep their culture intact in another country.

This is a map of Hmong populations in Asia, showing the tendency of the Hmong people to be spread out, which is especially true of Hmong diaspora. Hmong people tend to live in rural areas, far away from urban influence. This leads to Hmong communities developing different attributes. There are a few dialects of the hmong language, and some have developed scripts for their language rather than the more common romanized script. Hmong people refer to their last name as a "clan," and there are different clans depending on which area they live in. Those living in America, for example, are limited to 18 clans, with 12 making up the vast majority of the population.



Hmong people have some especial difficulty with maintaining their religious customs in a diaspora. Younger Hmong people, similar to young people of other immigrant ethnicities, tend to adopt cultural aspects of the country they live in, and distance themselves from their parents. Traditional shamanism includes many family-centered rituals, and the separation between the older and younger family members makes it difficult to work together as a family. Many younger hmong people are not supportive of their parents' religious traditions. The same goes the other way as well; many older hmong people are hesitant to accept anything from another religion and prefer their traditional belief, sometimes distancing themselves from christian family members because of it.
